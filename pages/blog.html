#Part One

Mixed origins - no child can choose the place and date of own birth

/* First draft: Mo, Feb 23, 2023 // Updated: Th, Nov 09, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Hi everyone,


I'm Nick Balan. I was born and raised in the capital city of Rep. Moldova - Chișinău. Also, in the same city, I completed my master's degree and then, right after graduation, moved to Romania for two years. After that, I moved to Germany, where I have lived for over six years.


My parents were born in the USSR, but my grandparents were born in Greater Romania. "How is this possible?" you might ask.


According to history, in 1940, the Soviet Union (USSR) issued an ultimatum to Greater Romania (currently Romania) requesting the cession of Bessarabia (the current Republic of Moldova) and Northern Bukovina. Thus, the region of Bessarabia (the present Republic of Moldova) and Northern Bukovina was annexed to the USSR, remaining part of it for half a century until 1991, when the Republic of Moldova declared its independence from the USSR.


That being said, when I was born in Chisinau, this city was part of the USSR at the time, and therefore, I was initially a citizen of the USSR, but this country dissolved a few years after I was born. Thus, in 1991, the Chisinau parliament declared its independence, the Romanian language was declared the official state language, and thus Moldova became independent.


Then, after completing my master's degree in Chisinau, I regained my Romanian citizenship, the legal premise resulting from my grandparents being Romanian citizens. So I am a citizen of both Moldova and Romania at the same time, which is confirmed by both my identity documents and my genealogical roots.


So, as a baby and as a result of historical events, I automatically became one of the first citizens of Moldova. It should also be mentioned here that, in Moldova, I went to kindergarten, high school, and university, all taught in Romanian.


Then, after completing my master's degree in Chisinau, I regained my Romanian citizenship, the legal premise resulting from my grandparents being Romanian citizens. So, I am a citizen of both Moldova and Romania at the same time, which is confirmed by both my identity documents and my genealogical roots.


I hope this brief historical summary explains my mixed origins in some way or another.

#Part Two

Moldova - my country of origin, though not my country of destination

/* First draft: Mo, July 17, 2023 // Updated: Mo, Nov 13, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Moldova is the country where I was born, where I grew up, where I finished my master's degree. It's where most of my relatives, childhood friends, and former master's classmates are.


Moldova is the place of my childhood and student life, the place that I love and hate simultaneously, in a ratio of 50/50. Moldova is where I had many happy moments but more unhappy moments. I can say that I know so much about what it's like to live in a country that hasn't woken up from the dream of the Soviet empire and that it potentially would take me more than a lifetime to write novels on this topic. That's why I will happily skip this dramatic part of the story and tell you just the positive side of it.


You won't believe that the most beautiful moments of my life in Moldova were things that would seem trivial to some of you. However, they were the only happy moments and are foundation stones for me: 1. All the happy moments with my family, relatives, and close friends. 2. When I had the opportunity to study at the university with extraordinary lecturers, PhDs in the field, and amazing colleagues; 3. When I had the chance to participate in the TEMPUS student exchange program at the Hochschule für Technik Stuttgart (2012); 4. When I happily completed my master's degree in Geoinformatics (2013), 5. When I finally regained Romanian citizenship (2014);


These were the most remarkable moments of my life in Moldova. I will always be grateful to the extraordinary people from Chișinău with whom I interacted throughout this period. Many more beautiful moments in my life followed just after I left Moldova, but I will tell you about them on another occasion.


Thus, after 2014, I decided to leave my parental home and find a purpose in the world, so I moved to Romania for two years and then to Germany, where I have lived since then until now. I do not intend to return to Moldova because every travel to Chisinau, even for three days, makes me feel unhappy by remembering all the drama I lived there. Also, my professional and personal goals are no longer directly related to my country of origin — more details about this topic in subsequent posts.

#Part Three

Romania - my grandparents' country of origin, and yet not my country of destination

/* First draft: Th, Aug 31, 2023 // Updated: Tu, Nov 14, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Romania is the country that I consider the mother country of my homeland - Rep. Moldova. My grandparents were born in Romania, so I am also Romanian, genetically speaking. Also, the region of Bassarabia, today known as Rep. Moldova, was forcibly separated and annexed to the USSR by a war ultimatum.


Thus, from my subjective point of view, Moldova and Romania should be diplomatically united within the European Union (UE). Significantly, the Romanian language is the state language of both countries, and both countries have many common Romanian traditions.


Although Romania is not a role model EU country for other European countries, it could be the best role model for Moldova's EU integration. If Moldova would reach at least the level of Romania's EU integration, it would be extraordinary, but there is still a long way to go.


Unfortunately, Moldova kept getting lost on its UE path, taking one step forward and one step back. Thus, for three decades of independence from the USSR, Moldova remained in the same place, pacing and moving in circles around its stagnation. Only now, citizens in Moldova still need to decide whether they want to join the EU or non existing USSR. Some of them still seem to like the USSR of the past, even though the USSR doesn't exist anymore.


Also, from my purely subjective point of view, Romania and Moldova would do well to take inspiration from Germany and look for a diplomatic solution for reunification within the EU. Especially now, with the war in Ukraine, this would be the best possible solution for both countries' economic and social development. Plus, from a historical point of view, they have always represented only one country, a country unified by the Romanian language and tradition.


You may ask now why I don't consider Romania my country of destination; it's simple - I am waiting patiently for the reunification of Romania and Moldova within the EU, and after that, if necessary, I will rethink my country of destination. However, such an event would take years or even decades.


By the way, Germany also did a reunification 30 years ago, which only strengthened it. Imagine, by aberration, how the EU would have been now if Germany had not reunified in 1990. Would those two German countries have been separated as strongly as the current reunified Germany is? I'm sure not; social division only leads to decline and degradation.


With all my heart, I wish to see the reunification of Romania with Moldova within the EU. Suppose current generations will not get a chance to witness a reunification. In that case, I hope that future generations will be able to accomplish what our current generations may not be able to accomplish.

#Part Four

Germany - is not my country of origin; however, I consider it my country of destination

/* First draft: Tu, Oct 3, 2023 // Updated: Th, Nov 16, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Germany is the country that inspired me and continues to inspire me ever since I visited it for the first time in 2012 as part of a student exchange at Stuttgart Technology University of Applied Sciences (HFT Stuttgart). This exchange of students lasted only three months, the most beautiful months of my life up to that point.


Also, in 2012, I was impressed by the German educational, social, and economic systems and how the German state creates conditions for society to develop and prosper nationally and within the European Union (EU). The German academic, economic, social, and legal systems are well established and enable German society to explore its potential to the maximum possible extent.


However, I want to be understood correctly: the German system is not perfect but very close to perfection. This world has no ideal system, and any system has advantages and disadvantages. Thus, I will highlight the advantages and disadvantages of the German, Romanian, and Moldovan systems in future articles.


At the same time, after six years of living in Germany, I am fortunate to have the opportunity to be part of German and EU society. Although I realize I will never be considered a German, it doesn't bother me at all; I know who I am and where I come from, and I have no reason to hide my unfair background. The past is past and can not be changed, so I'm working on improving my present and future. It is much more important for me to have the opportunity to be part of and contribute to a German and EU society. On the other hand, Germany is also considered one of the role model countries for any EU nation.


At the moment, Germany is the place where I want to develop myself professionally and personally and contribute as much as possible to both German and European society. Although my relatives and friends from Moldova and Romania find it strange that I don't want to relocate to my country of origin, I don't consider it abnormal.
