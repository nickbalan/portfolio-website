Part I

Mixed origins - no child can choose the place and date of own birth

/* First draft: Mo, Feb 23, 2023 // Updated: Tu, Aug 29, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Hi everyone,


I'm Nick Balan; I was born and raised in Chisinau, Moldova; in the same city, I completed a master's degree, then moved to Romania for two years; after that, I moved to Germany, where I have been living for more than six years.


My parents were born in the USSR, but my grandparents were born in Greater Romania. "How is this possible?" you might ask.


According to history, in 1940, the Soviet Union (USSR) issued an ultimatum to Greater Romania (currently Romania) requesting the cession of Bessarabia (the current Republic of Moldova) and Northern Bukovina. Thus, the region of Bessarabia (the current Republic of Moldova) and Northern Bukovina was annexed to the USSR, remaining part of it for half a century until 1991, when the Republic of Moldova declared its independence from the USSR.


That being said, when I was born in Chisinau, this city was part of the USSR at the time, and therefore I was initially a citizen of the USSR, but this country dissolved a few years after I was born. Thus, in 1991, the Chisinau parliament declared its independence, the Romanian language was declared the official state language, and thus Moldova became independent. So as a baby and as a result of historical events, I automatically became one of the first citizens of Moldova. It should also be mentioned here that, in Moldova, I went to kindergarten, high school, and university, all taught in Romanian.


Then, after completing my master's degree in Chisinau, I regained my Romanian citizenship, the legal premise resulting from my grandparents being Romanian citizens. So I am a citizen of both Moldova and Romania at the same time, which is confirmed by both my identity documents and my genealogical roots.


I hope this brief historical summary explains my mixed origins in some way or another.

Moldova is the place of my childhood and student life, the place that I love and hate simultaneously, in a ratio of 50/50. Moldova is where I had many happy moments and unhappy moments. Part III

Romania - my grandparents' country of origin, and yet not my country of destination

/* First draft: Th, Aug 31, 2023 // Updated: Tu, Sep 26, 2023 */
/* Author: Nick Balan // Category: Motivation */


Romania is the country that I consider the mother country of my homeland - Moldova. Romania is the country where my grandparents were born. Romania is the country of origin from which Moldova was forcibly separated and annexed by a war ultimatum to the USSR. Thus, from my subjective point of view, Moldova should be part of Romania or at least diplomatically united with Romania within UE. Significantly, the Romanian language is the state language of both countries, and both countries have many common Romanian traditions.


Although Romania is not an EU model country for other European countries, it could be a role model for Moldova's EU integration. If Moldova would reach at least the level of Romania's EU integration, it would be extraordinary, but there is still a long way to go. Unfortunately, Moldova kept getting lost on its UE path, taking one step forward and one step back. Thus, for three decades of independence from the USSR, Moldova remained in the same place, pacing and moving in circles around its stagnation. Until now, there are still citizens in Moldova who have not yet decided whether they want to join the EU or USSR. They still seem to like the USSR of the past and would like it to return, even though the USSR doesn't exist anymore.


From my purely subjective point of view, Romania and Moldova would do well to take inspiration from Germany and look for a diplomatic solution for reunification. However, it is a known fact that politicians from both countries do not even want to hear about an official reunification. Regardless of their opinion, as a citizen of both countries, I believe this is the best solution for both countries' economic and social development. Plus, from a historical point of view, they have always represented only one country, a country unified by the Romanian language and tradition.


You may ask now why I don't consider Romania my country of destination; it's simple - neither Romania nor Moldova has yet concluded that the only solution for prosperity and development is a reunification of Moldova with Romania. Although, I also consider that the most diplomatic solution for the Union of Moldova with Romania is the EU, even if it does not represent the official union, it will still be the best symbolic union of two Romanian states within the EU. By the way, Germany also did a reunification 30 years ago, which only strengthened it. Imagine, by aberration, how the EU would have been now if Germany had not reunified in 1990. Would those two German countries have been separated as strongly as the current reunified Germany is? I'm sure not; social division only leads to decline and degradation.


I wish with all my heart to seize during the years of life that God has reserved for me on this earth to see the reunification of Romania with Moldova within EU. Suppose current generations will not get a chance to witness a reunification. In that case, I hope that future generations will be able to accomplish what our current generations may not be able to accomplish.
